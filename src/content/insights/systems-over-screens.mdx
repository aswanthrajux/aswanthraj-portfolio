There's a tension at the heart of design work that nobody talks about honestly: **the things that look impressive in a portfolio are not the things that make the biggest impact in a product.**

A stunning screen. A beautifully crafted onboarding flow. A delightful microinteraction. These things win Dribbble likes and Behance features. They're also almost certainly not the most valuable thing a designer can work on.

The most valuable thing? A system.

## Why Systems Beat Screens

When you design a screen, you solve one problem. When you design a system, you solve a class of problems — including ones you haven't encountered yet.

I built the InMobi Design System in 2019–2020. Over the following three years, the team shipped 40+ product features using that system. My single investment compounded at 40×. No individual screen I could have designed over that period would have generated anything close to that leverage.

This isn't an argument against craft. It's an argument for **where** to apply craft. Craft at the system level propagates everywhere. Craft at the screen level stays on that screen.

## The Failure Mode

Most design system initiatives fail because they're framed wrong from the start. They're built as *repositories* — places to put components after they've been designed. Not as *products* — things with users, adoption, and continuous improvement.

The symptoms of a repository mindset:
- The system is maintained by one person (usually the most senior designer)
- Contributions require approval from the maintainer
- The system lags behind product needs, creating shadow systems
- Usage is mandated ("you must use the system") not earned

A product mindset asks different questions:
- Who are the users of this system? What are their jobs-to-be-done?
- What's the highest-friction workflow we can remove?
- How do we measure adoption? What does success look like?
- How do we handle backwards compatibility when we improve a component?

## The Three Layers of a Design System

After building and maintaining systems at two companies, I think about design systems in three layers:

**Layer 1 — Foundations:** Tokens. Typography. Colour. Spacing. The raw material from which everything else is built. Get this wrong and every component built on it is fragile. Get it right and the system scales almost automatically.

**Layer 2 — Components:** The building blocks. Buttons, inputs, cards, modals, navigation patterns. The goal at this layer is coverage of the 80% — the patterns that appear in every product. Not the 20% of bespoke, complex UI.

**Layer 3 — Patterns:** Composed experiences. Sign-in flows. Empty states. Error handling. Onboarding patterns. This is where system thinking meets product thinking — and where the highest-leverage design decisions live.

Most teams focus on Layer 2. The greatest value is in Layer 1 (because it propagates) and Layer 3 (because it solves real user journeys, not just UI atoms).

## The Adoption Problem

You can build a perfect system. You still have to sell it.

The hardest part of the InMobi design system wasn't building it. It was the two engineers who kept building components from scratch because "the system doesn't have exactly what I need." It was the designer who kept overriding system fonts because "the system just doesn't feel right for our product."

The strategies that worked for me:

**Eat your own dog food.** I rebuilt the design system documentation site using the design system. Every component was stress-tested in a real product environment before I asked anyone else to use it.

**Fast feedback loops.** Any designer could submit a "gap request" and I committed to a 2-week turnaround. The system felt responsive, not bureaucratic.

**Celebrate usage.** When a team shipped something fast using the system, I told the whole design org. Positive reinforcement is underrated in internal platform adoption.

**Accept imperfection.** A system in use with 70% adoption beats a perfect system with 10% adoption. Ship it. Iterate.

## What I Believe Now

A design system is the most leveraged product a designer can ship. It's not glamorous. It doesn't win awards. But it makes every other designer on the team faster, more consistent, and more confident.

The best system I ever saw didn't have the most components. It had the most clarity. Every designer who touched it knew exactly what the system was for, what it wasn't for, and where to go when the system didn't have what they needed.

**Clarity is the product.**
