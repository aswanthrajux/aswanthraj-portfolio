Dan Norman, in *The Design of Everyday Things*, wrote: *"Good design is actually a lot harder to notice, because it fits our needs so well that the design becomes invisible."*

I've been thinking about this for 15 years. But I didn't truly understand it until I started designing for the lock screen.

## When There Is No UI

The fundamental challenge of lock screen design is that the user hasn't opted in to an experience. They glanced at their phone to check the time. They may have 0.8 seconds of attention to spare — if you're lucky.

In that context, every UI principle I'd accumulated over a decade stopped applying. Navigation patterns? Irrelevant. Affordances? You have no interactions. Onboarding? There is no onboarding.

What I was left with was the question: **what is the most honest expression of value possible in the smallest possible footprint?**

This is what I now call the zero-UI challenge. Not no interface — but an interface that gets out of its own way completely.

## The Ambient Computing Shift

We're in the middle of a paradigm shift that most designers aren't paying attention to: the move from **intentional computing** to **ambient computing**.

Intentional computing: I pick up my phone. I open an app. I accomplish a task.

Ambient computing: The information finds me. I receive value without initiating an interaction.

Glance TV, Glance Hub, FolderWall — everything I've worked on at Glance is ambient computing. The user hasn't typed a query. They haven't opened an app. The product has to earn attention it was never explicitly granted.

This changes design in profound ways.

**The hook matters more than the flow.** In an app, you can design a user journey. In an ambient surface, all you have is the first 1.5 seconds. If you don't earn attention in that window, you've lost.

**Intrusion is the enemy.** The ambient surface has to add value without subtracting attention from whatever the user was doing. The moment your design demands attention rather than rewarding it, you've failed.

**Context is everything.** An app can be designed for a general state. An ambient surface is experienced in hundreds of micro-contexts: waiting for coffee, walking, distracted at work, bored on a commute. The design has to work across all of them.

## What This Taught Me About Design

Building for ambient surfaces radicalized my views on clarity.

I used to think clarity meant clear hierarchy, clear labels, clear calls to action. That's a necessary but not sufficient definition.

True clarity is when the interface knows its own role in the user's life. A TV screensaver isn't an app. It doesn't need a search bar. A lock screen isn't a notification centre. It needs to serve your current moment, not your stored requests.

The most common design mistake I see is **feature addition as a response to user needs.** User can't find something → add navigation. User misses notifications → add a notification layer. The surface grows. The experience degrades.

The counter-intuitive move is subtraction. **What can this surface afford to remove?** What's the minimum that delivers the maximum value?

## The Invisible Standard

I now evaluate my own design work against a test I call the invisible standard.

*If this design is working perfectly, will the user notice it at all?*

For a TV content card: no. They'll notice the story. The card should disappear.

For a mobile app flow: probably not. They'll accomplish the task. The flow should be frictionless.

For a design system: definitely not. The product built from it should shine. The system should be invisible infrastructure.

This isn't nihilism about design. It's the highest standard I know. Making something well enough that it disappears — that the human experience shines through undistorted — that's the craft.

Dan Norman was right. The best design is invisible design. But getting to invisible takes more work than getting to visible. It requires removing things, which is always harder than adding them.

**Invisible is the hardest thing to make.**
