In 2017, I ran my first design sprint. It was a disaster.

Not because the output was bad. Because I didn't know what success looked like. I facilitated five days of post-its, sketches, and prototypes. We built something. We tested it. Users were confused. We went back to our desks and… kept building the confused version, because we had a deadline.

I had failed to understand the point of the sprint.

## The Only Metric That Matters

Design sprints — and really, all of lean UX — are not about generating solutions. They're about reducing the time between having an idea and knowing if that idea is right.

**Time to validated learning** is the metric. Everything else is secondary.

This reframe changed how I ran every subsequent sprint. The question isn't "did we design something good?" It's "did we learn something true?" And the follow-up question is: what's the minimum work required to learn that thing?

## Lessons From 50+ Sprints

Over seven years at InMobi and Glance, I've run or facilitated over 50 design sprints across DSP interfaces, mobile surfaces, TV products, and design systems. Here's what I know now that I didn't know in 2017.

### Lesson 1: Compress ruthlessly

The original Google design sprint is 5 days. I've never run a 5-day sprint. My standard format is 2.5 days: half a day of problem framing, a day of ideation and prototyping, a day of user testing and synthesis.

The constraint is the point. When you have 5 days, you fill 5 days. When you have 2.5 days, you make decisions faster and the team stays energised.

### Lesson 2: The prototype is a question

Most teams treat the sprint prototype as a solution to validate. Wrong frame. The prototype is a set of questions made tangible. Before I build a single screen, I write out the three things I need to learn from the test. If the prototype doesn't test those three things, it's the wrong prototype.

### Lesson 3: Bad tests reveal bad questions

When a user test session goes sideways — when users are confused in ways you didn't anticipate — the natural response is to say "the prototype wasn't good enough." Almost always, the problem is the question. You were testing the wrong thing.

I now treat every confusing user session as a gift. It means I was wrong about what mattered. Finding that out in a sprint is infinitely cheaper than finding it out in production.

### Lesson 4: The sprint team is the deliverable

The decisions made in a sprint are important. The shared understanding built during the sprint is more important.

When a sprint team — designers, PM, engineers, and a customer support rep — spends 2.5 days working on the same problem together, they develop a shared mental model. That shared model means that when the sprint is over, everyone is building toward the same vision.

I've watched sprints produce "wrong" prototypes that led to right products — because the team alignment was so strong that they course-corrected quickly. I've also watched "right" prototypes shipped incorrectly because the team didn't share the same understanding of why the decisions were made.

### Lesson 5: Document aggressively

The sprint ends. The documentation often doesn't get written. And then, three months later, someone asks "why did we make this decision?" and nobody remembers.

I now require a sprint debrief document within 48 hours of every sprint. Three sections: what we tried, what we learned, what we're going to do next. One page. Non-negotiable.

## The Meta-Lesson

Fail fast, learn fast isn't a process. It's a **mindset.**

The process is the sprint, the prototype, the test, the synthesis. The mindset is being genuinely more interested in learning than in being right.

That mindset is hard. Especially when you're good at your craft. You become attached to your designs. You want to be validated, not challenged.

The best designers I've worked with all share this quality: they are **delighted** when they are wrong. Because being wrong means they found the truth, and finding the truth early is the highest-leverage thing a designer can do.
