*"AI didn't make me faster. It made my gaps visible."* — a note I wrote to myself after the first month of seriously using AI in my design workflow.

That's the uncomfortable truth about AI as a design tool. It doesn't just accelerate the work you're already good at. It also ruthlessly exposes the work you've been doing on autopilot.

## What Changed for Me

I've been building AI-native products at Glance for the last three years. Content recommendation systems, ambient intelligence layers, predictive app surfacing. Products where the AI is the product, not a feature bolted on.

But it took me embarrassingly long to bring AI *into* my design process itself.

The shift happened when I started using Claude and GPT-4 not to write copy, but to think. I'd describe a UX problem in natural language and ask it to challenge my framing. Not for answers — for better questions.

**Prompt engineering is a design skill.** The same thinking that makes you a good UX researcher — clear framing, exploring edge cases, avoiding leading questions — makes you effective at working with AI. Most designers who say "AI isn't useful for design" just haven't learned to prompt well yet.

## What AI Does Well in Design

**Rapid synthesis:** Throw 20 user research notes at an AI and ask for themes. Not perfect, but it gets you to 80% in 20 minutes. What used to take me a day of affinity mapping now takes an afternoon.

**Design critique:** Describe your design rationale and ask the AI to argue against it. Steel-man the critique. This forces you to either strengthen your argument or spot a real flaw before users do.

**Content at scale:** Variant headline copy, microcopy explorations, error message alternatives. AI generates, designer edits. The cognitive ratio shifts in your favour.

**Documentation:** "Here are the design decisions I made on this project. Help me write the design spec." First draft in 10 minutes. This matters because documentation is the highest-leverage design output most designers skip.

## What AI Cannot Replace

**Taste.** The ability to recognise quality, to know when something is right not because of a heuristic but because of cultivated aesthetic sensibility. This is built over years of exposure and reflection. AI has no taste — it has pattern-matching at scale.

**Context.** AI doesn't know that your product's users are 60-year-old factory managers in Chennai who use feature phones. It doesn't know the political dynamics on your team that make a certain solution impossible even if it's optimal. Context is everything.

**Embodied understanding.** The best UX insight I've ever had came from watching a user *struggle* — physically, emotionally — with a flow I'd designed. That watching is irreplaceable. No amount of synthesised research replaces direct human observation.

## The Designer's New Job

The question isn't "will AI replace designers?" The question is "what is a designer's unique contribution once AI handles the templatable work?"

My answer: **designers become the humans who know what good feels like, and know how to get teams to agree on it.**

The judgment layer. The taste layer. The facilitation layer. These are distinctly human, and they become *more* valuable as AI handles more of the production work.

The trap is using AI to go faster on work that shouldn't be done at all. The opportunity is using it to free up time for the work that only you can do.

## A Framework I've Settled On

I now think about AI assistance in three modes:

1. **Generate:** Let AI produce options. Review, filter, recombine.
2. **Challenge:** Use AI to pressure-test assumptions and design rationale.
3. **Document:** Use AI to capture, structure, and communicate decisions.

Mode 4 — **decide** — remains human. Always.

The designers who'll thrive in the next decade aren't the ones who resist AI. They're the ones who understand exactly where the human ends and the machine begins, and are ruthlessly thoughtful about that boundary.
