<SectionEyebrow>The Situation</SectionEyebrow>

## This wasn't a redesign. It was a rethinking of what a TV screen is allowed to do.

Glance Ambient is a TV-native experience that activates when the screen goes idle, no app launch or remote input required. Distributed across Airtel India and DirecTV US, it runs on roughly 3.7 million devices.

The original brief was straightforward: news, sports, wallpapers. Content to fill dead air. Commerce was never part of the plan.

<SectionEyebrow>Problem &amp; Reframe</SectionEyebrow>

## Users were present. But not participating.

TVs were on for three to four hours a day. Users were physically in the room. But they weren't watching. They were present without intent. Interaction rates were near zero. The content was perfectly fine. That was the problem.

<Pullquote>Passive attention does not create intent.</Pullquote>

We stopped asking "what content should we show?" and started asking "what would make a user actually act on a TV surface?" The shift that followed:

<SectionEyebrow>The Insight</SectionEyebrow>

## Fashion wasn't the obvious choice. It was the right one.

It's personal. It's visual. And unlike news or sports, it's emotionally immediate. People respond to clothes the way they respond to mirrors. When we brought virtual try-on to the ambient screen, something shifted.

<MediaBlock
  src="https://youtu.be/WSvag_rU-04"
  alt="V2: AI-generated fashion try-on on TV screen"
  caption="V2. The moment users saw themselves styled by AI on a TV screen."

Users weren't just looking at the TV. They were looking at themselves on the TV. That reaction (the pause, the lean-in, the "wait, that's me") told us what the product actually needed to be. Not a content channel. A surface where people could see themselves, styled by AI, at a moment when no one was selling anything to them. That's when the commerce potential became real.

<SectionEyebrow>The System</SectionEyebrow>

## A loop, not a feature.

Most users never complete the full loop, so every stage has to stand on its own.

Every stage was designed to work without the next one, because most users exit the loop early, and that still needed to feel complete.

<SectionEyebrow>Key Decisions</SectionEyebrow>

## What we chose, and why.

<DecisionCards decisions={[
  {
    title: "Removed News",
    body: "News had no action loop on a commerce surface. Informing and buying are different mental modes, and trying to serve both diluted both."
  },
  {
    title: "Identity over Utility",
    body: "Users don't engage with products; they engage with themselves. Fashion try-on was the entry point precisely because it made the screen personal before transactional."
  },
  {
    title: "AI before Control",
    body: "We needed to validate that users would act before adding the complexity of prompts. Adoption had to come first, or the controls would have no foundation."
  },
  {
    title: "Automation at Scale",
    body: "800+ non-repetitive cards per day was never achievable with manual curation. AI had to own the feed, or the system would collapse under its own content debt."
  },
  {
    title: "Mobile as Bridge",
    body: "A TV surface has a ten-foot problem. The phone became the primary interaction device for a screen you sit across the room from."
  }
]} />

<SectionEyebrow>What Didn't Work</SectionEyebrow>

## Three things that are still honest problems.

<FailureList items={[
  {
    title: "Prompt Overload",
    body: "When we first shipped the prompt interface, users stalled. Too many options created a blank-canvas problem on a surface that should feel effortless. We pulled it back to three default prompts, and completion rates improved immediately. AI-powered doesn't mean user-controlled."
  },
  {
    title: "Weak Commerce Conversion",
    body: "The try-on worked. The checkout didn't. Users would explore enthusiastically, generate looks, refine their choices, then hesitate at the point of purchase. The AI created desire. The commerce handoff broke the moment. This is still an open problem, and probably the most important one left to solve."
  },
  {
    title: "Latency",
    body: "A 30-second generation delay is acceptable in a mobile app. On an ambient TV screen, it kills the mood entirely. We shipped skeleton states and moved to async generation to mask the wait, but real-time output remains the actual goal. We're not there yet."
  }
]} />

<SectionEyebrow>V4 / What's Next</SectionEyebrow>

## V3 proved users would act on prompts. V4 asks what happens when you remove the noise.

V3 also created a new problem. A crowded L0 where multiple prompts compete for the same moment of attention. The screen that was once passive had become busy.

V4 explores a different direction. A cleaner surface. One focused entry point that takes the user deeper into an agentic flow, rather than asking them to choose between options.

The architecture is shifting from a single-agent model to a multi-agent system. Multiple specialised agents, each capable of guiding users through a focused, category-specific journey.

This is concept work. Not publicly released.
