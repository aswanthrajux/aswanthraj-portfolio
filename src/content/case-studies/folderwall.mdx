## The Challenge

The average Android user has 80 apps installed but regularly uses fewer than 10. The rest sit in folders, forgotten, or are discovered only when the user has a specific need and remembers the app exists.

This isn't a user problem. It's an **information architecture problem.** Apps are organised by how they were installed, not by how they're used. Folders are manual, static, and personal. They don't adapt, learn, or recommend.

FolderWall was our answer: an AI-powered app organisation and discovery system that automatically groups apps into contextual collections and surfaces them at the right moment.

## My Approach

I joined the FolderWall project when it was still called "Smart Folders" and had a narrow scope: just auto-create folders based on app category.

My first contribution was to question the frame. **Folders aren't the insight. Context is.** The useful question isn't "what category is this app?" but "when would you want to see this app?"

This led to the core design principle: **FolderWall organises by situation, not by category.**

## Design Process

### Research: How People Actually Use Apps

We ran a three-week study with 60 participants using passive app-usage tracking (with explicit consent). The data was revealing:

- Apps cluster into 6–8 "situations" for most users: morning routine, commute, lunch break, work, evening wind-down, weekend leisure
- 72% of app launches are habitual: the same apps at the same times
- 28% of launches are exploratory, trying to remember "that app I haven't used in a while"

FolderWall had to serve both: support the habitual 72% with speed and familiarity, and unlock the exploratory 28% with smart recommendations.

### Defining the "Wall" Metaphor

The original "Smart Folders" metaphor was too conservative. Folders imply hierarchy and manual organisation. We wanted something more fluid.

After three rounds of conceptual development, we landed on the **Wall** metaphor: a living display surface that reorganises itself based on context. Like a physical notice board that someone has helpfully curated for you, pinned with the things you need right now.

This metaphor guided every design decision: cards instead of list items, spatial arrangement instead of alphabetical lists, ambient updates instead of jarring reorganisations.

### Prototyping the Recommendation Engine UX

The hardest design challenge was communicating AI-driven recommendations without being creepy. "We know you're near a restaurant right now" feels invasive. "Here are apps for where you are" feels helpful.

The difference is entirely in the design language. We developed three principles:

1. **Show, don't tell.** Surface the app. Don't explain why unless asked.
2. **Offer, don't impose.** Recommendations are suggestions. The user's own organisation always takes precedence.
3. **Control is always visible.** A single tap on any app reveals a "Why is this here?" explanation.

### Solution

FolderWall ships as a home screen replacement layer (accessible from the lock screen via Hub). The visual structure has three zones:

**Primary Zone: Recent & Predicted.** The apps you're most likely to use right now, based on time, location, and usage history. Displayed as large, tappable cards.

**Secondary Zone: Situational Groups.** Automatically created collections like "Morning Routine," "Work," "Weekend," or custom user-created groups. Each group has 4–12 apps and updates dynamically.

**Discovery Zone:** Apps you haven't opened in 30+ days, surfaced with a "Rediscover" prompt. Often the highest-value recommendation layer for users who've lost track of what they have.

## Impact

- **App engagement breadth:** FolderWall users open 2.3× more unique apps per week than users on standard Android launchers
- **Discovery rate:** 41% of users opened at least one "rediscovered" app per week
- **Retention:** FolderWall users have 28% higher 90-day device retention (users are less likely to switch phones when their experience feels personalised)
- **NPS:** 78, our highest product NPS score across the Glance suite

## What I Learned

**The hardest UX is designing behaviour change.** FolderWall asked users to give up manual folder management, a deeply habitual behaviour. We couldn't win with features alone. We had to demonstrate value on day one, then earn the deeper behaviour change over weeks.

**AI recommendations need a UX contract.** We defined a "recommendation contract" with users early: we will only suggest apps based on your own usage patterns. We will never use third-party data. We will always let you override. This contract was never explicitly shown to users, but it guided every micro-decision in the design.
