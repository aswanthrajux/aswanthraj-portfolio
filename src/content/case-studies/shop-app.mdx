<SectionEyebrow>The Situation</SectionEyebrow>

## Samsung said yes. Nobody knew what to do with it.

The deal was straightforward on paper. Samsung would pre-install a Glance app on their smart TVs. Not a screensaver. A full, first-party TV app with direct placement in the home experience.

That's unusual. Most OEM agreements lock partners into specific surfaces with fixed behaviors. This one didn't. Samsung handed us a blank canvas on the most passive screen in the home.

We had no product definition. No existing behavior to build on. No user expectation to meet. Just an audience, a remote, and an open question: what should this be?

<SectionEyebrow>The Direction</SectionEyebrow>

## We explored everything. Then we came back to what we are.

The first round of thinking was wide. Games. Ambient extensions. A second-screen companion. TV-native utility apps.

Every direction ran into the same wall: we had no platform advantage in any of them. We'd be building from scratch against players who'd been doing it for years.

The honest question was: what does Glance actually know how to do?

Commerce. Specifically: surfacing the right product to the right person at the right moment.

That wasn't a fallback. It was a decision. One that eliminated everything else and made the real problem visible.

<SectionEyebrow>The Problem</SectionEyebrow>

## People don't shop on TV. That's not a gap in the market. It's a behavior problem.

There's no established pattern for TV commerce. No user expectation. No muscle memory. Nobody opens a TV app thinking: I should buy something.

The screen is passive. The remote is slow. The attention is often shared. And intent doesn't exist at the moment the screen turns on.

This is fundamentally different from mobile commerce, where the device is personal, interaction is fast, and shopping is already habitual.

<Pullquote>A UI can't solve a behavior problem. You have to design the behavior first.</Pullquote>

We weren't designing a shopping app. We were designing a reason to shop.

<SectionEyebrow>The Strategic Shift</SectionEyebrow>

## Catalog-based shopping was the wrong answer.

The obvious move: build a product catalog. Categories. Filters. Search. A digital shelf.

That model assumes the user arrives with intent. On a TV surface, they don't. Browsing requires effort. A remote is not a cursor. And attention on a TV is always a negotiation between whoever's in the room.

Catalog-based shopping collapses the moment you remove intent.

So we didn't build a catalog. We built an agent.

The difference isn't cosmetic. A catalog waits for the user to navigate. An agent starts a conversation. One puts friction at the beginning. The other removes it entirely.


<SectionEyebrow>The System</SectionEyebrow>

## Not an interface. A guide.

The shopping agent isn't a search bar with better results. It's a system that understands what the user wants, asks when it doesn't, and gets progressively better at not needing to ask.

<SectionEyebrow>The Interaction Model</SectionEyebrow>

## Two layers. No more.

TV interaction has a hard constraint: the remote. Five directional buttons and a select. That's the vocabulary.

We designed around it rather than against it.

The system has two interaction layers. Primary: the agent responds, renders, and leads. Secondary: prompt-based direction changes. The user doesn't navigate to options. They say where they want to go.

Voice is available as a fallback for users who prefer it. Not the primary input. Most TV users won't speak to their TV. The system works for both without forcing either.

Keeping it to two layers was a choice, not a limitation. Every additional layer adds a decision point. On a passive screen with a slow input device, decision points are the enemy of completion.

<SectionEyebrow>Agent Memory</SectionEyebrow>

## The agent gets better the more you use it.

The first session requires context-gathering. The second one doesn't. The agent retains style preferences, rejected categories, price sensitivities, and brand affinities across sessions.

This isn't just personalisation. It's the mechanism that makes TV commerce viable long-term. A catalog stays static. An agent evolves.

The asymptote is a system that knows what you want before you say it. We're not there yet. But every session moves the system closer.

<SectionEyebrow>The Landing Page Problem</SectionEyebrow>

## An agent is useless if nobody opens the app.

This was the harder problem. Not the commerce experience. The entry point.

Why would someone open a shopping app on their TV? They opened the TV to watch something. There's no natural bridge between "I want to watch" and "I want to shop."

The standard answer: personalisation. Show people what they like. The problem: we had no personal data at launch. Privacy constraints, cold start, zero behavioral history.

So we asked a different question.

<Pullquote>People don't come to TV to shop. They come to watch. What if shopping started from watching?</Pullquote>

<SectionEyebrow>The Landing Page Strategy</SectionEyebrow>

## Shop what you watch.

The entry point isn't "here are products you might like." It's "here are products from what's trending."

We mapped product associations to trending shows and movies. The content people were already watching became the discovery surface. A crime drama surfaces watches and outerwear. A reality cooking show surfaces kitchen gear. A travel documentary surfaces luggage and accessories.

None of this required personal data. Trending content is public signal. Product association is editorial intelligence. The system combines both.

The result: a landing experience that feels relevant before the agent knows anything about you. It creates immediate context without relying on behavioral tracking. And it solves the cold start problem without asking the user to do any work.

<SectionEyebrow>Distribution Strategy</SectionEyebrow>

## Vision AI as a channel, not a feature.

The app solves the experience. But the app still has to be opened. That's the distribution problem.

Samsung's Vision AI layer changes the equation. It surfaces contextual prompts based on what users are actively watching. Not a notification. Not an interruption. A prompt tied to content the user already cares about.

We proposed using Vision AI as the primary entry channel for the shopping app. Not through banner placement or home screen promotion. Through content context.

**Strategy 1: Watch and interact.**

The user is watching Top Gun. Vision AI surfaces prompts: outfits from the movie, related items. The user taps. The agent starts. No app switch. No context loss.

Commerce layers on top of viewing. The user can browse, interact with the agent, and explore products without leaving what they were watching. The TV does two things at once. Most importantly: the user didn't decide to shop. The moment created the decision.

  caption="Watch and interact. The agent activates from a content prompt. Viewing continues."

**Strategy 2: Contextual app entry.**

Some users want to go deeper. When they tap the Vision AI prompt, they reach a dedicated entry point for Glance. From there, if they choose to open the app, the context travels with them.

The app opens preloaded. Not with a generic catalog. With products mapped to what they were watching. Top Gun surfaces aviation jackets, sunglasses. The entry point carries intent into the app. The user doesn't have to rebuild the context they already had.

This is the difference between an app that receives traffic and an app that receives ready-to-act users.

<SectionEyebrow>Challenges</SectionEyebrow>

## The honest version.

**No behavior exists.** We were designing a behavior from scratch on a platform with no precedent. There's no research to cite. No benchmark to beat. Users didn't know what to expect because nothing like this existed before.

**Remote input is brutal.** Two interaction layers is the right answer. Getting there required removing a lot of things the team wanted to keep. Every cut was a negotiation between product ambition and interaction reality.

**The trust gap is real.** Users engage with the agent. They explore. They try on. They hesitate at checkout. TV commerce doesn't have the credibility that mobile commerce spent a decade building. That can't be shortcut. It has to be earned.

**QR breaks the session.** The mobile bridge is the right architecture. But any transition to a second device is a friction point. Not every user completes it. The ones who don't lose the try-on experience entirely.

**Agent design is hard.** Deciding what the agent says, when it asks, when it assumes, and how it fails gracefully is a product problem as much as a technical one. The iteration surface is enormous.

<SectionEyebrow>What Worked</SectionEyebrow>

## Three things that moved.

**The agent reduced friction at entry.** Users didn't have to navigate. They could start with a word and see something relevant. That's a meaningful shift on a surface where navigation is the primary barrier.

**Try-on built confidence.** Users who completed the QR bridge were meaningfully more likely to continue toward purchase. Seeing yourself in a product changes the decision calculus. The abstraction collapses when it's your face.

**Contextual commerce drove engagement.** The trending content landing page created immediate relevance. Users engaged with content-mapped products even before the agent learned anything about them. The non-personalised entry strategy held.
